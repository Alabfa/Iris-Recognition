4.1

Introduction — Iris Anatomy & Uniqueness

Multilayered Structure of the Iris

1

Posterior Layer

Two cells thick, heavily colored epithelial cells — impenetrable to light

2

Muscle Layer

Sphincter & dilator muscles — contracts/dilates the pupil in response to light

3

Stromal Layer

Collagenous connective tissue + blood vessels (radial direction) — source of MOST texture

4

Anterior Border Layer

Foremost visible layer; increased density of chromatophores — imaged by camera for recognition

Anatomical regions (inner→outer): Sclera · Ciliary Zone · Collarette · Pupillary Zone · Pupil

Why the Iris is a Powerful Biometric

  • Texture arises from random morphogenesis during prenatal growth → each iris is unique
  • Even monozygotic (identical) twins exhibit different iris textures
  • Left iris ≠ Right iris of same person (epigenetic, not genetic)
  • Recognition uses TEXTURE detail — NOT iris color
  • Texture is a phenotypic trait with limited genetic penetrance
⭐ Key: Recognition is based on the anterior iris texture (muscles + stromal + border layer) captured via NIR camera
4.1

Iris Zones, Texture Features & Color

Iris Zones

Pupillary Zone

Inner zone, surrounding the pupil. Contains radial furrows. More stable texture.

Ciliary Zone

Outer zone extending to the limbus boundary. Contains contraction furrows (concentric lines visible when darkly pigmented).

Collarette

Circular zigzag ridge separating the two zones. Crypts (Fuchs crypts) appear mainly around it.

Crypts (Fuchs)

Pit-like irregular structures that allow fluids to enter/exit during pupil dilation. Visible when anterior layer thins.

Key Texture Features

  • Crypts — Pit-like irregular holes; main in collarette area
  • Contraction Furrows — Concentric lines in ciliary zone; deepen when pupil dilates
  • Radial Furrows — Radial lines in pupillary zone
  • Rings — Concentric rings around pupil
  • Vasculature — Blood vessels in stromal layer
  • Freckles — Localized pigment deposits
Color is genetically determined (melanin) but NOT used in recognition — only the anterior texture detail matters

4.3

Image Acquisition

Sensor & Setup

  • Monochrome CCD camera sensitive to Near-Infrared (NIR) spectrum
  • NIR illumination: wavelength 700 nm – 900 nm
  • External NIR light source co-located with camera
  • Captures series of images → quality-based selection
  • Most systems require cooperative subjects at close range (10–15 inches)

Why NIR? (Two Key Advantages)

① Texture Visibility

Dark irides absorb visible light → RGB images do not clearly reveal texture. NIR penetrates the anterior iris layer, revealing hidden textural detail.

② Non-Intrusive

NIR is invisible to the human eye → image acquisition causes no discomfort; safe when illumination power is properly controlled.

Resolution Requirement

  • 100–200 pixels across the iris in the radial direction
  • Quality-selected subset (often 1 image) sent for further processing

Factors Degrading Image Quality

  • Partially closed eyelids
  • Intruding eyelashes (spurious edges)
  • Harsh or non-uniform illumination
  • Low resolution / large imaging distance
  • Extremely dilated or constricted pupil
  • Motion blur / defocus
  • Printed or fake contact lenses

Commercial Devices (Examples)

  • Panasonic BM-ET330 — portable, 11.8–15.7 inch range, dual-eye
  • LG IrisAccess 4000 — auto-focus, wall-mount, 10.2–14.2 inch
  • Oki IrisPass-M — wall-mount, 1–2 ft range
  • Retica MobileEyes — hand-held dual-eye (2.8 lbs)
  • IrisGuard IGH1000 — hand-held, 4.7–11.8 inch, 750 g

Advanced: Iris On The Move (IOM)

  • Acquires scans from moving subjects at considerable stand-off distance
  • Challenges: iris shifts slightly (hippus) within moving eyeball within moving head
  • Requires long-focal-length lenses + choke-point design
IOM systems represent the frontier — current research targets non-cooperative subjects under challenging illumination and large standoff distances
4.4

Iris Segmentation — Overview & Challenges

⚠ MOST CRITICAL MODULE — Inaccuracies in localizing the iris can SEVERELY impact matching accuracy and undermine system utility

Goal & Primary Task

  • Localize the spatial extent of the iris and isolate it from adjacent structures
  • Detect: INNER boundary (pupillary) & OUTER boundary (limbus/sclera)
  • Remove: pupil, sclera, eyelids, eyelashes from the iris region

Two Boundary Assumptions

  • Pupillary boundary: strong intensity contrast (pupil vs iris) → relatively easy to detect
  • Limbus boundary: contrast between iris and sclera → may be weaker in NIR; can be interrupted by eyelids
  • Assumption: both boundaries ≈ circles (though iris is not perfectly concentric with pupil)

Post-Processing Steps

  • Eyelid detection: search for parabolic edge within the outer circle (spline fitting)
  • Eyelash detection: search for strong near-vertical edges in the segmented iris
  • Pupil center is NASAL (toward nose bridge) and INFERIOR (below) the iris center — not perfectly concentric

Why Segmentation is Difficult

1

Stochastic Texture

The iris texture is highly irregular — it contains numerous "edge-like" features randomly distributed on its anterior surface. Simple appearance-based models cannot describe it.

2

Boundary Estimation Difficulty

The limbus boundary may not be sharp; over-/under-segmentation risks if boundary detection is inaccurate. Eyelid boundary is irregularly shaped (not circular).

3

Eyelash Occlusion

Eyelashes present in the iris image, generating spurious edges that interfere with boundary detection and texture extraction.

Methods: Integro-Differential Operator (assumes circular boundaries) | Geodesic Active Contours (handles irregular shapes)
4.4.1

Segmentation: Integro-Differential Operator

Equation (4.1)
max(r, x₀, y₀)   Gσ(r) * ∂/∂r   ∮ I(x,y) / 2πr   ds

Term-by-Term Explanation

I(x,y)Input eye image — pixel intensity at location (x,y)
(x₀, y₀)Center of the candidate circle
rRadius of the candidate circle
∮ dsLine integral along the circumference of the circle
∂/∂rRadial derivative — measures intensity change (edge strength) at each point
1/2πrNormalization factor by circle perimeter length
Gσ(r)Gaussian smoothing filter — blurs crypts, furrows, freckles to avoid spurious edges
max(r,x₀,y₀)Maximize sum → find the circle with the strongest edge → true iris boundary

Algorithm — Step-by-Step

  1. 1Apply Gaussian smoothing Gσ → remove noise from crypts, freckles, furrows
  2. 2Select a candidate circle: center (x₀,y₀) and radius r
  3. 3For all pixels on the circumference → compute radial gradient ∂/∂r
  4. 4Sum all gradient values along perimeter, normalize by 2πr
  5. 5Repeat for many (r, x₀, y₀) combinations
  6. 6Select parameters yielding MAXIMUM sum → strongest circular edge = iris or pupil boundary
  7. 7Detect PUPIL boundary first (strong contrast), then LIMBUS boundary
  8. 8For limbus: integrate only over near-vertical arcs (ignore eyelid portions)
Note: Pupil center is nasal (shifted toward nose) and inferior (below) the iris center — iris and pupil are NOT concentric
4.4.2

Segmentation: Geodesic Active Contours (GAC)

Motivation — Why GAC?

  • Integro-Differential Operator assumes CIRCULAR boundary — fails when eyelids create irregular non-circular shapes
  • GAC uses active contours (snakes) that can detect irregular boundaries of ANY shape
  • GAC combines energy minimization (classical snakes) + geometric active contours (curve evolution)

Level Set Formulation

ψ(x,y) = signed distance from curve γ(t)

ψ = 0 → ON the curve  |  ψ < 0 → INSIDE  |  ψ > 0 → OUTSIDE

Curve γ(t) is the zeroth level set of the embedding function ψ

Evolution Equation (4.4)
ψₜ = −K(c + εκ) |∇ψ| + ∇ψ · ∇K

Parameters

KStopping function: decelerates curve evolution near the boundary
cInflation velocity: pushes curve outward toward object boundary
εControls smoothness of level sets
κCurvature of level sets: provides smoothing constraints
∇KGeodesic term: attracts curve toward boundary even with gaps in K

GAC Algorithm Steps

  1. 1Detect pupillary boundary first (Integro-Differential or other method)
  2. 2Initialize contour γ(t=0) as a circle just beyond the pupillary boundary
  3. 3Initialize ψ as signed distance function to γ(0) (cone shape)
  4. 4Compute stopping function K(x,y) = 1/(1 + |∇(G*I)|^α / k^α)
  5. 5Modify K: delete pupillary boundary edges → prevent premature stopping
  6. 6Evolve ψ using Equation (4.7) iteratively (Δt = 0.05, c=0.65, ε=1)
  7. 7Upwind finite differences for velocity term (avoids singularities)
  8. 8Monitor thin plate spline energy: terminate when contour stabilizes
  9. 9GAC can SPLIT at local minima (crypts, thick radial fibers) and MERGE → handles local minima unlike parametric methods
  10. 10Final contour = true limbus boundary
GAC vs IDO: IDO assumes circles (simple, fast) | GAC handles ANY shape (more accurate, more complex). Parameters: c=0.65, ε=1 for all iris images
4.5

Iris Normalization — Daugman's Rubber Sheet Model

Purpose of Normalization (3 Reasons)

(a) Pupil Size Variation

Pupil contracts and dilates with illumination → changes the spatial extent of the iris texture; normalization removes this effect

(b) Cross-Population Consistency

Pupil size varies across individuals; normalization maps all irides to a common image domain of the same size

(c) Rotational Alignment

Two normalized irides can be registered by a simple TRANSLATION (shift) operation → accounts for head tilt during acquisition

Daugman's Rubber Sheet Model — Remapping Equations

I(x(r,θ), y(r,θ)) → I(r,θ)

x(r,θ) = (1−r)·xp(θ) + r·xl(θ)
y(r,θ) = (1−r)·yp(θ) + r·yl(θ)

r ∈ [0,1]  ,  θ ∈ [0,2π]
xp, ypPoints sampled from the pupillary boundary
xl, ylPoints sampled from the limbus boundary
r=0Pupillary boundary  |  r=1 → Limbus boundary
RowsAngular direction in original iris
ColumnsRadial direction in original iris

Binary Noise Mask (Generated Alongside)

  • Label = 1 → valid iris pixel  |  Label = 0 → noise pixel (eyelid / eyelash)
  • Mask is also unwrapped (normalized) alongside the iris for faster matching
  • When using GAC: irregular boundary converted to circular estimate; excluded pixels added to mask
Normalization converts the annular iris region from Cartesian (x,y) to pseudo-polar coordinates (r,θ) — the so-called 'unwrapping of the iris'
4.6

Iris Encoding — 2D Gabor Wavelets & Iris Code

2D Gabor Wavelet
G(x,y) = e−π[(x−x₀)²/α² + (y−y₀)²/β²] · e−2πi[u₀(x−x₀) + v₀(y−y₀)]

Encoding Pipeline

  1. 1 Convolve with 2D Gabor Wavelet
    Multi-resolution filter applied to the normalized iris image. Wavelet in polar coords:
    H(r,θ) = e−iω(θ−θ₀) · e−(r−r₀)²/α² · e−i(θ−θ₀)²/β²
  2. 2Complex Phasor Output
    Convolution yields complex values (real + imaginary) at each pixel location. Magnitude and phase are both computed.
  3. 3Use PHASE only — Ignore Magnitude
    Phase is robust to illumination changes; magnitude varies with contrast. Only the phase quadrant is encoded → better generalization.
  4. 4Quantize to 2 bits per pixel
    Phase quantized based on quadrant in complex plane (4 quadrants = 2 bits):
    hRe = sign(Re{…})  |  hIm = sign(Im{…})
  5. 5Result: Iris Code
    2D binary matrix (common dimension: 2048 bits). Entire iris texture represented as a compact binary code.

Matching — Hamming Distance

Formula
HD = [ (IrisCodeA ⊕ IrisCodeB) ∩ MaskA ∩ MaskB ]
        ────────────────────────────────
                  MaskA ∩ MaskB
⊕ (XOR)Detects all bit disagreements between two iris codes
∩ (AND)Masks noisy / invalid regions (eyelashes, eyelids)
DenominatorNormalizes total number of valid bits compared → HD ∈ [0,1]
HD = 0Perfect match (identical iris codes)
HD ≈ 0.5Two different (random) irides → bits differ ~50% by chance
RegistrationBefore computing HD: shift iris codes to align (head tilt correction)
⭐ Why phase not magnitude? Phase is illumination-invariant | Why Gabor? Iris texture exists at MULTIPLE scales → wavelet multi-resolution analysis is ideal

Iris Image Quality Assessment

Quality evaluation is based on factors that DEGRADE or REDUCE the usable iris region. 8 Key Degradation Factors:

(a) Occlusion
(b) Defocus
(c) Motion Blur
(d) Non-uniform Illumination
(e) Low Resolution
(f) Iris Dilation
(g) Off-Angle Imaging
(h) Printed Contact Lenses
1

Gradient-Based Sharpness

1/w = S / (Mᵢ − Mₚ)

Region of interest: pixels near pupillary boundary. Compute median intensities Mp (pupil) and Mi (iris). Collect top 20 absolute horizontal gradients → average = S. Sharpness: 1/w = S/H where H = Mi−Mp. Threshold: 1/w > 0.5 → good quality.

3

Local Fourier Spectral Analysis

D = [ (F₁+F₂+F₃) ; F₂/(F₁+F₃) ]

Two 64×64 local regions selected from iris. Quality descriptor D = (F₁+F₂+F₃ ; F₂/(F₁+F₃)) where Fi is power in low/middle/high frequency bands. Focused image: high F₂/F₁+F₃ ratio. Mean of two local descriptors = final quality measure.

2

Fourier High-Frequency Energy

Dσ(μ,ν) = exp(−(μ²+ν²)/σ²) · F(μ,ν)

Defocusing attenuates highest frequencies (exponential decay). Measure total power at high spatial frequencies in 2D Fourier domain. Make measure image-content-independent: ratio of high-frequency band power to low-frequency band power.

4

Wavelet-Based Band Energy (Quality-Weighted HD)

HDw = Σ(EX·EY·XOR) / Σ(EX·EY)

Mexican hat wavelet CWT applied at 3 scales (s₁,s₂,s₃). Product response: wmul = w(s₁)×w(s₂)×w(s₃). Band energy Et = (1/Nt)Σ|wmul|². Quality index Q = (1/T)Σmt·log(Et) with Gaussian weights mt (inner iris weighted higher).

Key Insight: Good iris image = Sharp + Rich texture + High frequency content | Wavelet technique uses HIGHER weights for INNER iris (more stable, less occlusion)
4.8

Performance Evaluation & Real-World Challenges

Large-Scale Experimental Results 🔥

  • Dataset: 632,500 iris images from 316,250 individuals spanning 152 nationalities (Daugman)
  • Result: near-ZERO error rates are achievable — but ONLY with high-quality images
  • Quality of iris image must be strictly monitored to ensure textural clarity

NIST 2006 Performance Benchmarks

MetricDefinitionNIST Value
FMRDifferent persons matched as same0.1%
FNMRSame person rejected as different1.1% – 1.4%

Key Performance Takeaways

  • Iris texture is substantially diverse across the population → high discrimination power
  • Even monozygotic twin irides show structural differences → uniqueness is epigenetic
  • Performance is highly dependent on IMAGE QUALITY — poor images → higher FNMR
  • At HD threshold of ~0.32: optimal balance between FMR and FNMR

Real-World Challenges in Iris Recognition

Acquisition Difficulty

Iris is small + slightly oscillating (hippus) within moving eyeball within moving head → very hard to capture from distance

Image Quality Degradation

Motion blur, defocus, eyelashes/eyelids, non-uniform illumination, off-axis gaze → increases FNMR significantly

Off-Angle Imaging

Eye not looking directly at camera → geometric distortion; requires correction models

No Theoretical Uniqueness Proof

Iris uniqueness is demonstrated EXPERIMENTALLY — no rigorous mathematical proof of individuality exists yet

Distance & Non-Cooperative

Traditional systems require close-range cooperative subjects; Iris-On-The-Move still an open research challenge

Multimodal Integration

Combining iris with face, skin texture, or periocular region (eye surroundings) to improve accuracy

Security & Privacy

Large template databases risk data leakage and identity theft → Cancelable biometrics and privacy-preserving techniques needed

Applications: Airports (UAE expellee identification) | Coal mines (fingerprints impractical) | Border control | High-security access | Forensics
